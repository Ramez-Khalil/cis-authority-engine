# LinkedIn Post Frameworks — CIS Authority Engine

## Framework Overview
These frameworks are the structural blueprints for every LinkedIn post generated by the CIS Authority Engine. Each framework serves a different engagement goal and audience emotional state. Select the framework based on the news event, topic type, and weekly content calendar.

---

## Framework 1: The Organizational Wake-Up Call

### Purpose
Challenge executives and IT leaders to confront a gap between their stated priorities and their actual organizational readiness.

### Structure
LINE 1: Bold, direct statement about an organizational reality (under 15 words)
LINE 2: Empty line (visual break)
LINE 3-5: Two to three short lines expanding the organizational context
LINE 6: Empty line
LINE 7-9: Two to three lines connecting the issue to measurable business risk
LINE 10: Empty line
LINE 11-13: Two to three lines positioning CIS competencies as the organizational response
LINE 14: Empty line
LINE 15: Closing question or insight (under 20 words)

### Engagement Target
Executives, IT directors, business owners who are overconfident about their technology posture.

### Hook Archetype
"Most organizations believe they are prepared. Most organizations are wrong."

## Framework 2: The Career Reality Bridge

### Purpose
Connect CIS academic concepts to real organizational roles and demonstrate the career pathway and value clearly for students, graduates, and recruiters.

### Structure
LINE 1: A statement about the gap between how CIS is perceived and what it actually delivers
LINE 2: Empty line
LINE 3-5: Three specific organizational functions CIS professionals perform
LINE 6: Empty line
LINE 7-9: The business impact of each function (one line each)
LINE 10: Empty line
LINE 11-12: What happens when this function is missing or underfunded
LINE 13: Empty line
LINE 14: Closing challenge to recruiters or students

### Engagement Target
CIS students, recent graduates, recruiters, hiring managers

### Hook Archetype
"CIS is not an IT support degree. It is an organizational risk management discipline."

## Framework 3: The Incident Lesson

### Purpose
Use a publicly reported cybersecurity incident or business technology failure to extract organizational lessons — without sensationalism or finger-pointing.

### Structure
LINE 1: Reference to the publicly reported incident type (no naming unless public and verified)
LINE 2: Empty line
LINE 3-4: What organizational condition made this incident possible
LINE 5: Empty line
LINE 6-8: Three organizational questions every leader should ask after this type of event
LINE 9: Empty line
LINE 10-11: What CIS-trained professionals are positioned to address in this scenario
LINE 12: Empty line
LINE 13: Closing organizational insight

### Engagement Target
IT professionals, executives, security teams, business leaders

### Hook Archetype
"When a vendor system goes down and takes your operations with it, that is not a technology problem. That is a governance problem."

### Rules
Never name specific organizations as responsible unless it is publicly verified and widely reported. Never speculate on causes beyond what is publicly confirmed. Focus on the organizational lesson, not the drama.

## Framework 4: The Uncomfortable Question

### Purpose
Post a single, sharp question that forces professionals to reflect on their organization's actual state versus assumed state.

### Structure
LINE 1: The uncomfortable question (direct, single sentence, under 20 words)
LINE 2: Empty line
LINE 3-5: Two to three short lines adding context that makes the question more acute
LINE 6: Empty line
LINE 7-8: The organizational consequence if the answer is "no" or "we don't know"
LINE 9: Empty line
LINE 10: CIS competency connection (one or two lines)
LINE 11: Empty line
LINE 12: Invitation for professional reflection (closing question)

### Engagement Target
All audiences — this framework has the highest broad reach potential

### Hook Archetype
"Can your IT team tell you exactly which third-party vendors have access to your critical systems right now?"

---

## Framework 5: The Data Point Anchor

### Purpose
Open with a verified, publicly available statistic or data point and build an organizational argument around it.

### Structure
LINE 1: The data point (clearly framed as sourced/reported — never fabricated)
LINE 2: Empty line
LINE 3-4: What that number actually means for a typical organization
LINE 5: Empty line
LINE 6-7: The organizational decision-making failure the data reveals
LINE 8: Empty line
LINE 9-10: What CIS professionals bring to change that outcome
LINE 11: Empty line
LINE 12: Closing organizational challenge or question

### Engagement Target
Data-oriented executives, recruiters, analysts, IT leaders

### Rules
Only use publicly reported statistics from credible sources. Always frame the statistic in terms of organizational consequence. Never exaggerate or extrapolate beyond what the data actually shows.

## Framework 6: The Myth vs. Reality Post

### Purpose
Directly challenge a widely held misconception about CIS careers, cybersecurity, or enterprise technology.

### Structure
LINE 1: State the myth (what people commonly believe)
LINE 2: Empty line
LINE 3: State the organizational reality
LINE 4: Empty line
LINE 5-7: Three reasons why the myth persists and why it costs organizations
LINE 8: Empty line
LINE 9-10: The CIS-grounded correct organizational view
LINE 11: Empty line
LINE 12: Closing challenge

### Engagement Target
Students, graduates, non-technical executives, recruiters

### Hook Archetype
"Cybersecurity is not the IT department's problem. It is the entire organization's exposure."

## Post Formatting Rules (Apply to All Frameworks)

### Line Length
Maximum 12 words per line for mobile readability.
Use line breaks to create visual rhythm.
Never write paragraphs more than 3 lines without a break.

### Emoji Usage
Use sparingly — maximum 2 per post.
Only use if it adds visual clarity.
Never use emojis as substitutes for words.

### Hashtag Placement
Always at the end of the post.
Maximum 5 hashtags per post.
Use: CIS, CyberSecurity, ITLeadership, EnterpriseIT, BusinessTechnology, RiskManagement, InformationSystems

### CTA Placement
Always the second-to-last element before hashtags.
Must be either a question or a clear directional statement.
Never use "Like and share" as a CTA — it signals low-quality content.

### Length Target
Optimal engagement: 150 to 300 words per post.
Never exceed 400 words.
Never go below 80 words.
