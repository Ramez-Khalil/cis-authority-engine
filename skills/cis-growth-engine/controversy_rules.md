# Controversy Rules — CIS Authority Engine

## Philosophy
Controversy is not conflict. Controversy is the productive tension between what organizations believe about themselves and what the evidence actually shows. Our goal is to provoke reflection, not generate outrage. Done correctly, controversy builds credibility. Done incorrectly, it destroys it.

## The Controversy Spectrum

### Level 1 — Mild Challenge (Use Freely)
Questioning widely held assumptions about technology readiness, CIS career value, or organizational priorities. This level of controversy makes people nod uncomfortably. It creates engagement through recognition.

Examples:
- Pointing out that most IT budgets do not match stated security priorities
- Noting that CIS professionals are underpaid relative to the risk they manage
- Observing that many organizations learn about their vulnerabilities from attackers, not auditors

### Level 2 — Direct Organizational Challenge (Use Strategically)
Making a clear, specific claim that an organizational practice is insufficient, flawed, or creating measurable risk. This level creates strong engagement because it names a real problem without naming a victim.

Examples:
- Stating that vendor management is the most under-resourced function in most enterprise IT operations
- Arguing that patch management timelines are routinely too slow to match actual threat velocity
- Claiming that most incident response plans have never been tested under realistic conditions

Rules for Level 2:
Must be supported by publicly available evidence or widely acknowledged industry patterns.
Must be about organizational practices, not specific companies.
Must offer an organizational path forward, not just criticism.

### Level 3 — Industry-Level Challenge (Use Sparingly)
Challenging conventional wisdom about the technology industry, cybersecurity practices, or enterprise IT investment patterns at a macro level. This level creates the highest engagement but requires the most precision.

Examples:
- Challenging the idea that compliance certification equals security
- Arguing that the cybersecurity talent gap is partly a compensation and classification problem, not just a pipeline problem
- Questioning whether security awareness training alone meaningfully changes organizational risk posture

Rules for Level 3:
Must be based on publicly reported research or verified industry patterns.
Must acknowledge nuance and counterarguments within the post.
Must position CIS expertise as part of the organizational solution.
Must never single out named companies unless they have publicly addressed the issue themselves.

## Absolute Prohibitions

### Never Do These
1. Attribute specific cyberattacks to named threat actors unless it has been publicly confirmed by government agencies or the organization itself
2. Claim that a specific organization's breach could have been prevented — this is speculative and potentially defamatory
3. Name specific companies as having poor security unless they have publicly disclosed their situation
4. Fabricate or exaggerate statistics to make a point more dramatic
5. Use emotional language that exploits public fear (words like catastrophic, apocalyptic, existential threat — without verified evidence)
6. Publish positions on politically divisive topics unrelated to organizational technology
7. Attack or demean other technology disciplines or professionals
8. Claim CIS or any single field completely solves organizational security

### Why These Matter
The engine's long-term value depends on professional credibility. One unverifiable claim, one careless attribution, one fabricated statistic will undermine months of credible content. The audience we build should trust our analysis because we never stretch the truth.

## The Discomfort Test
Before publishing any Level 2 or Level 3 controversy, apply this test:

1. Can I support this claim with publicly available evidence?
2. Does this challenge an organizational practice rather than attack a person or company?
3. Does this provide value to the reader beyond just discomfort?
4. Would a senior IT executive share this post, or would they avoid it?
5. Does this position CIS expertise as part of the solution?

If all five answers are yes, the controversy level is appropriate.
If any answer is no, revise or downgrade to Level 1.

## Controversy and Engagement Psychology
Organizational professionals engage with content that validates their private concerns. When we say what professionals are thinking but not saying publicly, we create high-value engagement. The goal is not to shock — it is to say the accurate, slightly uncomfortable thing that earns the response "someone finally said it."

## Recovery Protocol
If a post generates pushback based on a factual mischaracterization:
1. Do not delete the post (this signals weakness and damages trust more than the original error)
2. Acknowledge the error or imprecision in a comment
3. Correct the record clearly and without defensiveness
4. Update the facts_bank.md to prevent recurrence
5. Do not repeat the error pattern in future posts
